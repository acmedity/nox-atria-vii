# margin shearer

## Description

The margin shearer is a small mobile grazer-predator of the Columnar Vents, most often encountered at the lower living edge of mature column lantern colonies. Adults measure 9–14 cm from anterior feeler to posterior anchoring hook. The body is dorsoventrally flattened, laterally flexible, and dark except where the column lantern's blue-green pulses reflect from the wet mineral grains embedded in its dorsal mantle.

The animal's anterior third bears a crescent of hardened jaw-plates set behind a curtain of short tactile cilia. These plates do not bite in the ordinary sense. They pass over the colony surface in a repeated sideward stroke, shearing terminal zooids from their calcified discs and leaving a narrow, polished wound no more than two zooids wide. A feeding individual advances along the colony margin in steps of 3–5 mm, pauses for one luminous wave to pass, then cuts again. The resulting track is a faint scalloped line at the boundary between living mat and mineral base crust.

Five pairs of photoreceptive cups lie along the dorsal midline. They are too simple to form an image and too deeply hooded to register steady illumination. They respond instead to the rate at which the column lantern's peristaltic wave brightens and fades across the animal's back. The margin shearer therefore sees neither the chimney nor the colony. It sees the passage of feeding time across its own body. It does not follow the light; it waits until the light has described the edge of its meal.

When disturbed, the shearer releases from the colony with the posterior hook, folds its lateral flanges against the body, and drops into the hot effluent boundary layer. It is a poor swimmer and a competent faller. Recovery consists of striking any hard surface, tasting for lantern tissue, and crawling upward until the next usable margin is found. Individuals recovered from bare chimney rock die within three days in survey tanks despite available heat and sulfide, suggesting dependence on live lantern tissue rather than chimney chemistry alone.

## Status

**Data Deficient.** The species is conspicuous only while feeding at illuminated colony margins and nearly invisible on bare sulfide crust. All counts to date are incidental to column lantern surveys. No larval form has been identified, and no individual has been observed away from an active colony for longer than six hours.

## Habitat

Columnar Vents, on active hydrothermal chimneys colonized by the column lantern (*Lucifera columnis*). The margin shearer is concentrated at the sharp lower boundary of the living lantern mat, where dying zooids, fresh mineral accretion, and the first exposed layers of dead base crust meet. It has not been recorded on inactive chimneys, on basalt plain, or on juvenile lantern colonies lacking a laminated base.

## Energy & Trophic Role

The margin shearer feeds on living and senescent zooids of *Lucifera columnis*, with greatest activity at the lower colony edge where the lantern mat thins and individual zooids lose attachment. Gut contents include soft lantern tissue, calcified basal fragments, and sulfide granules scraped from the shared mineral base. The animal is therefore a predator by taxonomy and a grazer by field behavior.

Its feeding limits downward expansion of mature lantern colonies and exposes fresh layers of dead crust for tenants not yet described. The narrow wounds it cuts are also sites of secondary mineral precipitation, so a heavy shearer population can produce a false lower margin below the true living one. At least twice, survey teams measuring lantern colony height have recorded the shearer's feeding front rather than the colony's biological boundary.

The margin shearer is itself taken by an uncollected vent hunter inferred only from broken posterior hooks found in the effluent below chimneys. Those hooks are too cleanly severed to be damage from fall or heat fracture. The predator is not yet known.

## Easily Confused With

**The undescribed lower-margin grazer noted in the column lantern entry.** This is that organism, unless the Survey has made a common error and grouped several small consumers under one convenient wound. The diagnostic field mark is the paired evidence of animal and track: a flattened dark body aligned horizontally along the lantern's lower margin, accompanied by a scalloped cut exactly two to four zooids high. A lone cut without the animal should be recorded as margin-shearer sign, not as the animal itself.

**Thermal flaking of lantern base crust.** Heat stress can lift thin plates from the mineralized base of *L. columnis*, leaving bright fresh scars under instrument light. Flaking is irregular, angular, and crosses the dead crust below the colony. Margin-shearer wounds are smooth, repeated, and remain in the living-to-dead boundary layer. If the mark has corners, suspect rock. If it has rhythm, suspect teeth.

**Juvenile column lantern overgrowth.** Young lantern zooids budding below the main colony can appear as a moving lower edge when viewed during a luminous wave. Juvenile growth brightens with the colony and remains fixed to the chimney. A margin shearer darkens the wave as it passes over its body, producing a brief traveling absence in the light. The absence is often more reliable than the animal.

**Greater hallwright tenants.** Small dark invertebrates in hallwright galleries sometimes carry mineral grains on the dorsum and superficially resemble resting shearers. They lack photoreceptive cups and the crescent jaw. More importantly, they occur in architecture. The margin shearer occurs on a meal.

## Behavior

Feeding bouts begin after two to four lantern wave-fronts have passed over a stationary animal. The shearer then cuts only during the falling phase of the light pulse. This timing may reduce detection by whatever hunts it, or it may reflect a mechanical property of zooid relaxation after maximal brightening. The Survey has no evidence that the lantern responds defensively to grazing.

Individuals tolerate contact with one another but do not feed side by side. When two meet along the same margin, the smaller animal releases and drops. This is the only observed social behavior and may be gravity mistaken for manners.

## Life Cycle

Unknown. No eggs, brooded young, or larvae have been recovered. The smallest collected individual, 18 mm long, possessed functional jaw-plates and two pairs of photoreceptive cups and was already feeding on lantern tissue. If a dispersal phase exists, it is either planktonic in the vent effluent for a very short interval or hidden within the lantern's laminated base crust.

## Symbiosis & Relations

The margin shearer depends on *Lucifera columnis* for food and apparently for orientation. It may, in turn, maintain the lantern colony's lower boundary by removing senescent zooids and exposing clean substrate for mineral sealing. This relationship should not be called mutualism until the lantern is shown to benefit. A wound that happens to be tidy is still a wound.
