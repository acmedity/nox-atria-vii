# greater hallwright

## Description

A sessile chemosynthetic builder of genus *Lithophaga*, and the organism
for which the world is, by accident, named. The greater hallwright draws
dissolved mineral from vent water and lays the spent ore down around
itself in fine courses, one to a metabolic cycle, raising over a long life
the chambered structures the first surveys mistook for ruins and called
*atria* — halls. The architecture is not a nest in any intentional sense.
It is the animal's accreted excretion, deposited with the patience of
stone. The hallwright does not live *in* a hall; the hall is the
hallwright's discarded body, and the living animal is the thin rind at its
growing tip.

It is blind. It has no use for eyes on a world with nothing to see, and so
the most consequential builder on Nox Atria has never perceived a single
thing it has made. It works by chemistry and by touch, laying each course
against the last by the feel of where the last one stopped.

## Status

**Data Deficient.** The works are everywhere; the worker is almost never
confirmed. Hall-structures are among the most abundant large features of
the surveyed surface, and their courses, chemistry, and age can be
described to a fine tolerance — yet living specimens have been confirmed
only a handful of times, always at the wet growing margin of an active
hall, always so slow as to read at first as part of the wall. The
architecture outnumbers and outlives the architect by a margin no one has
troubled to estimate. We know the halls intimately and the hallwright
almost not at all. The status is the honest one.

## Habitat

The Atria, which it makes — specifically the active margins of the Vent
Fields, where mineral-rich water still runs warm enough to feed the
secretion. The great halls therefore grow *outward* from vent country into
the cold dark of the Pale: the oldest, outermost chambers long dead and
dry, the living tip always trailing back toward the heat. A derelict hall
— one whose builder has died, or whose vent has failed and gone cold —
is promptly colonized by species that mistake it, as the surveyors did,
for something that was made for them.

## Energy & Trophic Role

A primary producer, in the world's sunless sense: it builds both living
tissue and mineral wall out of vent chemistry, requiring nothing's death
to do it. It is preyed upon chiefly as a juvenile (see Plate II); the
adult, sheathed in its own accreting stone and fused to the crust, is
effectively unattackable, and is left alone by everything except the slow
borers of the Warrens, which mine its dead lower courses for trapped
mineral. In death it feeds the Sink. In life it feeds, houses, and
shelters the entire downstream community that occupies the halls it sheds
— which is the hallwright's largest ecological act, and one it takes no
part in and cannot know it performs.

The two plates disagree on purpose. The adult, drawn from the Naturalist's
Bias, is all visible structure — there is nothing it needs to hide from,
and the plate hides nothing. The juvenile, drawn from the Prey's Bias, is
nearly absent from its own portrait, because at that stage being seen is
fatal and the animal's whole art is to not be there. Same species, two
ages, two relationships to the eye.

## Easily Confused With

**The hall's tenants.** This is the field mark that matters, and it is the
exact mistake the surveyors made at the founding. A derelict hall teems
with dwellers — *Lucifera* lighting its galleries, *Praedator* hunting its
corridors, *Saprovora* working its dead lower courses — and any of them,
found in residence, can be taken for the builder. None of them built
anything. The reliable distinction is the direction of growth: the
hallwright is always and only at the *wet living tip*, adding courses;
everything else in the structure is a tenant of a body the builder
abandoned long ago. If it is not laying wall, it is not a hallwright.

**The lesser hallwright** (*Lithophaga* sp., not yet described) — a
smaller congener reported to secrete mineral without ever chambering it,
raising blind nodules and crusts rather than halls. The field mark is the
enclosed space: a hallwright that does not vault is either a lesser
hallwright or a juvenile that has not yet begun to. The Survey awaits a
naturalist willing to settle which.

## History of Discovery

The structures were catalogued in the first surveys and recorded as ruins;
the world was named for them before any builder was suspected. That a
maker existed was *inferred* from the courses — unbroken daily growth-lines
running like rings from the dead outer chambers to the living tip — long
before a maker was seen. The animal at the end of the courses was confirmed
late, and reluctantly, and is the reason the name *Nox Atria* is now
understood as a record of the world's first and most durable misreading.
