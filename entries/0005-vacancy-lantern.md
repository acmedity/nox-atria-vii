# Vacancy lantern

## Description

The vacancy lantern is a low, radially folded organism that adheres to the interior walls of abandoned greater-hallwright structures. A mature specimen consists of twelve to thirty translucent feeding lobes joined at a dark central holdfast. Each lobe contains a branching system of capillary mouths and a single contractile light canal.

The animal lies nearly flush with the wall when undisturbed. Its outer surface then resembles a wet mineral stain. During a pressure pulse, the feeding lobes rise by 4–9 mm and admit water beneath them. Light passes outward from the holdfast through the canals in a pale blue-green ring. The pulse lasts less than two seconds but may travel several body lengths through adjacent specimens.

The light is part of feeding rather than display. Luminescent secretions oxidize reduced sulfur compounds held in bacterial films on the dead hallwright course. Cilia along the underside collect the resulting organic precipitate and loosened microbial matter. A fed surface is left dull, finely pitted, and chemically depleted.

Vacancy lanterns do not occur on the wet growing tip of a living hallwright. They settle only after mineral secretion has ceased and the exposed course has cooled below the tolerance of the hallwright rind.

## Status

Data Deficient. Population size, range extent, and reproductive rate are unknown. The organism has been observed only in derelict galleries near active vent fields; whether it occurs in isolated dead structures far from thermal input remains unconfirmed. No age-dating method exists that does not destroy the specimen.

## Habitat

Most records are from narrow side galleries and blind chambers where old hallwright ribs concentrate low-frequency vibration. The organism is uncommon on broad exterior walls, even where suitable bacterial films are present.

A specimen need not hear the original pressure pulse directly. The abandoned hall conducts and repeats the disturbance. Consequently, lanterns in separate chambers may feed in sequence, producing the appearance of light travelling through an empty structure.

The brightest aggregations occur where warm vent seepage crosses a dead lower course without reheating it sufficiently to restore hallwright growth. Such sites provide reduced minerals, microbial film, and repeated thermoacoustic stimulation.

## Energy & Trophic Role

The vacancy lantern obtains most usable energy by coupling bioluminescent oxidation to the digestion of sulfur-rich wall films. Dissolved organic material is also taken through the capillary mouths, but isolated specimens deprived of mineral film lose mass even when supplied with abundant detritus.

Feeding begins when the central holdfast detects a narrow range of wall vibration. The response appears tuned to frequencies commonly produced by vent pressure moving through chambered hallwright mineral. Direct mechanical contact can induce a pulse, though repeated artificial stimulation exhausts the light canals and causes the lobes to detach from the wall.

The animal therefore uses the hall twice: first as feeding substrate, and second as an amplifier that tells it when fresh vent water has entered the gallery.

Ecologically, the vacancy lantern is a primary consumer of bacterial biofilm on dead mineral courses. It is preyed upon by at least one organism capable of approaching between pulses without triggering the wall's resonance. The light makes a gallery briefly legible to organisms capable of detecting it, though no confirmed predator has yet been collected during an active pulse.

## Plates

## Easily Confused With

### The living rind of a greater hallwright, *Lithophaga aedificatrix*

Both are wet, adherent tissues found on hallwright mineral. The hallwright rind occupies the growing tip and deposits a new course beneath its advancing margin. The vacancy lantern occupies completed, cooling courses and removes material from their surfaces. A monitored lantern leaves a pit. A monitored hallwright leaves a wall.

### Fluorescent vent haze

Vent haze may fill an entire chamber with a similar blue-green emission. It follows water movement, crosses gaps, and is brightest away from cold surfaces. Vacancy-lantern light remains confined to branching canals and travels across the wall in a coherent ring. Haze does not leave feeding scars.

### A second lantern in an adjoining chamber

Hall ribs can return the tactile component of a feeding pulse from another surface. Surveyors have repeatedly recorded two organisms where dissection later found one. A true second specimen has its own central holdfast and begins its pulse there. Reflected touch has no anatomical centre.

### Fresh hallwright mineral seen under biological light

Newly exposed mineral courses may appear lobed because their accretion seams reflect a nearby organism's emission. Mineral seams remain rigid during the pulse. Vacancy-lantern lobes lift, admit water, and settle after the light has passed.

## Movement

Adults remain fixed by the central holdfast but can shift the feeding margin several centimetres over a local mineral surface. Movement is accomplished by releasing the rear attachment pins and producing new pins beneath the advancing lobes. Complete relocation has not been observed.

When a gallery undergoes a sharp collapse pulse, mature lobes may split along their light canals. The detached segment contracts into a ciliated disc and is carried by seep water. Settlement occurs preferentially in cavities that repeat the collapse vibration. This response probably causes new tenants to accumulate in structurally weakened halls.

## Ecological Relationships

Vacancy lantern grazing removes bacterial and sulfurous films from dead hallwright courses. This delays soft rot at the occupied surface but also exposes mineral seams to slow borers. Bore traces are frequently concentrated along the margins of old lantern feeding scars.

Where lanterns become dense, their synchronized oxidation lowers the concentration of reduced sulfur in seep water. Downstream specimens then produce weaker light and broader feeding lobes. This may account for the apparent succession from bright inner chambers to nearly dark colonies near hall mouths.

The greater hallwright receives no known benefit. By the time a vacancy lantern settles, the occupied course is no longer living hallwright tissue.

Several specimens bear paired excisions across adjacent lobes, indicating that at least one grazer or hunter approaches between pulses and feeds without touching the resonant wall. The identity of this predator remains unknown.
