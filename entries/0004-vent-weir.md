# vent weir

## Description

The vent weir is a sessile organism of the effluent boundary layer, the hot sleeve of exhalation that clings to the lower third of an active chimney below the living margin of a column lantern colony. It is anchored to bare chimney rock by a single mineralized holdfast and hangs across the thermocline: its upper body, an upturned intake funnel, sits in the hot, shimmering effluent, while its lower body and rejecting sphincter descend into the cold, still dark beyond the boundary. A mature individual spans 8 to 15 cm from funnel rim to sphincter, and its posture is not incidental. The animal is arranged to bridge two thermal worlds at once, because the bridge is how it feeds.

The funnel is the conspicuous structure: a shallow upward-opening cup, 4 to 9 cm across the rim, walled in soft translucent tissue and fringed with a dense border of short cilia and pores. The cup opens toward the lantern margin above it and receives whatever descends — heated effluent, suspended mineral particulate, sloughed lantern tissue, flakes of dead base crust, and, not infrequently, live fauna that has let go of the margin. The funnel is not a mouth in the muscular sense. It is a settling basin. Material collects in it and is worked slowly downward by the ciliary fringe into the gut.

The body's interior is dominated by the **thermogradient column**, a dense axial organ that runs the length of the animal from funnel base to sphincter. This is the metabolic engine and the reason the animal must straddle the boundary. The column maintains a steep internal temperature differential — hot at the crown, held in the effluent; cool at the base, held in the dark — and derives usable energy from the sustained gradient across it rather than from any chemical substrate. The mechanism is not resolved. The Survey's working description is a biological heat engine: a tissue arranged to run metabolism off a maintained thermal difference, in the way the column lantern's coordinated wave is a signal whose carrier we cannot name. The vent weir does not oxidize sulfide and does not fix carbon from the mineral. It eats the temperature.

Around and within the thermogradient column lies the gut lumen, a narrow void channel that receives funnel material and digests it against a glandular wall. Digestion is soft-tissue-first and unhurried; a captured animal is reduced from the outside in over a period the Survey estimates in days, not hours. What the gut cannot break down — chitin, most conspicuously — is not stored and not passed through the body. It is carried back to the sphincter at the base and expelled downward into the cold dark. The animal is a one-way filter for everything except the hard parts, which it returns.

## Status

**Data Deficient.** The vent weir was, until this session, not an organism at all but an inference — the presumed author of the clean-severed posterior hooks of *Praedator limenrasor* found scattered below colonized chimneys and catalogued in the margin shearer entry as the sign of an unknown vent hunter. Direct observation is recent and thin. The animal is dark, sessile, and positioned in a lethal thermal layer that survey instruments enter poorly; it is far easier to find its discarded hooks than its body. No count exists. No larval stage is known. The dependence on a stable thermocline of a particular steepness makes it, in principle, sensitive to any shift in vent output, but vent output on Nox Atria VII appears stable on the timescales the Survey can measure, which are not the timescales that matter.

## Habitat

Columnar Vents, in the **effluent boundary layer** — the vertical zone below the sharp lower margin of a *Lucifera columnis* colony, where the hot exhalation of the lower chimney shears against the cold optically null dark of the surrounding Pale. The zone is newly formalized in the Atlas; it was implicit in the margin shearer's habit of dropping "into the hot effluent boundary layer" when disturbed, and the vent weir is the organism that gives the layer an occupant rather than a mere gradient. Individuals require both a live chimney (for the hot side of the gradient) and proximity to the still, cold plain (for the cold side); they have not been found on chimneys whose effluent is too diffuse to hold a sharp boundary, nor on inactive chimneys, nor up in the lantern mat itself. The animal lives on the edge, in the exact sense.

## Energy & Trophic Role

**Primary strategy: thermal-gradient metabolism.** This is the first organism the Survey has assigned to *Thermovora*, the heat-feeders, and it defines the genus by living entirely off the temperature differential across the thermocline rather than off heat as a warm place to sit. It is sessile and slow, as the genus expects. Its living comes from the boundary layer itself; the boundary layer is the resource.

Predation, or what has been mistaken for it, is a supplement and not the trade. The vent weir's funnel catches fauna that falls from the living margin above — overwhelmingly the margin shearer (*Praedator limenrasor*), which drops into exactly this layer when disturbed and is, in the words of its own entry, "a poor swimmer and a competent faller." A shearer that fails to strike usable substrate on the way down strikes the weir's funnel instead, and is digested. This binds the two organisms into a relation the earlier entry inferred but misread: the shearer's clean-severed hooks are not the mark of a hunter that cuts them off. They are the chitinous parts the weir cannot digest and expels intact from its sphincter into the cold dark below — where they settle on the basalt plain and are found. The hook is not severed. It is handed back.

The vent weir therefore closes the open trail of the margin shearer entry ("something takes shearers in the effluent; follow the hook fragments downward") with a correction rather than a confirmation. The thing that takes shearers is not a predator in any behavioral sense. It hunts nothing, pursues nothing, and cuts nothing. It hangs still across a temperature boundary, eats the boundary, and occasionally receives a falling animal it did not seek and cannot fully keep. What eats the vent weir in turn is not known; its position in a lethal thermal layer likely shields it from most consumers, and no weir-sign has yet been found in any gut or any Sink fossil.

## Easily Confused With

**The unknown vent hunter of the margin shearer entry.** They are the same trail and not the same claim. The earlier entry recorded broken shearer hooks below chimneys, "too cleanly severed to be damage from fall or heat fracture," and inferred an uncollected predator that severed them. The vent weir is the source of those hooks, but it is not a hunter and it severs nothing. The diagnostic difference is the whole reveal: a severing predator would leave a *cut* hook, showing tool-marks or crush at the break; a weir-expelled hook is *complete* — the entire chitinous posterior, sphincter-polished and unbroken, dropped whole because it could not be digested. If the hook is broken, look for a hunter the Survey still has not found. If the hook is intact, you have found the weir's leavings, not a kill.

**Column lantern, *Lucifera columnis*.** Both are sessile organisms of the colonized chimney, and a careless survey pass at the wrong height might file the weir as a detached fragment of lantern colony. The distinctions are total. The lantern lives in the upper third and glows; the weir lives in the lower boundary layer and produces no light whatsoever. The lantern is a colonial mat of thousands of millimetric zooids; the weir is a single organism with a funnel, a gut, and a holdfast. The lantern is a primary producer feeding on sulfide chemistry; the weir feeds on the thermal gradient and eats no chemistry at all. The one field mark that settles it in the dark, where the glow is absent: the lantern has a mineralized *base crust* of laminated dead zooids, and the weir has a single *point holdfast* and no crust. Colony versus individual; light versus none.

**Terrace builder, *Lithophaga gradata*.** Distant, but worth the line, because both are sometimes described as "a stalked thing that builds a hard part and hangs fauna beneath it." The terrace builder is a *Lithophaga*: it mineralizes carbonate into stacked sealed shelves and feeds by chemosynthesis on seep methane, in the Cold Seep Talus, far from any vent. The vent weir mineralizes nothing but its holdfast, seals no chambers, and feeds on heat, in the Columnar Vents. A terrace builder's mass is the animal's own excreted stone; a vent weir's mass is soft tissue on a single mineral foot. If the structure is a tower of shelves, it is stone the animal made; if it is a funnel on a stalk, it is the animal itself.

**Abiotic thermal shimmer and mineral drape in the boundary layer.** The lower chimney develops sulfide draperies and shimmer-borne mineral films that, seen edge-on through effluent distortion, can suggest a soft body hanging from the wall. Drape is irregular, brittle, and continuous with the chimney's mineral surface; the weir is discrete, soft, and joined to the rock at a single point. The test is the funnel: drape has no upturned cup and catches nothing, while a weir catches, holds, and — over days — empties whatever fell into it.

## Behavior *(optional)*

The vent weir does not move, orient, pursue, or, so far as observed, respond to the passage of a lantern pulse across it the way the margin shearer does. Its only known dynamic behavior is at the sphincter, which expels an accumulated indigestible bolus — a shearer's hook, most often — at intervals that appear to follow completion of digestion rather than any external cue. A weir observed over one boundary-layer survey expelled twice; both expulsions were single intact hooks. The Survey notes, without hypothesis, that the animal's entire visible activity across a monitoring period was the letting-go of two hooks into the dark.

## Symbiosis & Relations

The relation to *Praedator limenrasor* is trophic and one-directional, and it is tempting to read it as the vent's disposal system: the lantern grows, the shearer grazes the lantern's lower margin and occasionally falls, and the weir receives the fall and returns the shearer's hard parts to the plain, closing a small local loop between the lantern's light above and the cold dark below. Whether the weir benefits the shearer population by any mechanism — thinning it, or merely consuming its accidents — is unknown and should not be assumed. A funnel that happens to receive a falling animal is not, on that evidence, part of the animal's life history. It is where some of them stop.
