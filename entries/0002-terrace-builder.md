# terrace builder

## Description

A sessile colonial metabolist that fixes seep carbonate into ascending stacked shelves. The living animal is not the stone; it is the thin film of tissue at the growing rim of the topmost shelf, where it oxidizes methane against the brine and lays down a fresh course of mineral beneath itself as excreted waste. Having built a shelf, it climbs onto it and builds the next. The colony is therefore a record of its own ascent: each terrace is one growth season, narrowing as it rises because the methane flux thins with distance from the seep foot.

The interior of an old terrace is hollow. As the animal abandons a course, the lumen channels through which it once circulated fluid seal at their throats and dry into void chambers. A mature colony is mostly empty inside — closed rooms it built and left, stacked into a tower it can no longer feel.

The whole surface holds a biofilm sheen, thickest at the active foot and thinning to nothing on the dead upper shelves. This is the greasy, flint-smelling film that coats the entire Cold Seep Talus; *L. gradata* is one of its principal secretors. To stand in this biome is, in part, to stand inside this organism's exhalation.

## Status

Data Deficient. Colonies are easy to find and impossible to age without destroying — counting terraces requires the cut face, and the cut kills the foot. We do not know whether a tall colony is old or merely well-fed, whether shelves are annual or opportunistic, or whether the animal at the rim is one individual extended over decades or a succession of short-lived rim-tissues inheriting an address. The structure outlives every method we have of questioning it.

## Habitat

Carbonate talus on the down-slope side of brine pools, rooted in rubble (the seep foot), oriented gravity-true into the dark brine column above. The colony grows toward no light and away from no predator; it grows simply upward, following nothing but the failing supply of gas from below. Above the active rim the water is barren — energy comes from beneath, so the colony's own height is a journey away from its food.

## Energy & Trophic Role

**Primary producer by chemosynthesis.** *L. gradata* oxidizes seep methane and fixes carbon into both tissue and mineral. It feeds the biome twice:

- **As substrate:** the abandoned lower terraces are colonized by borers, tenants, and rot-feeders. The void chambers shelter small fauna that could not survive exposed on the talus.
- **As sink:** when a colony's seep foot finally exhausts its gas, the whole tower stops, the biofilm dies back, and the carbonate slowly returns to the Sink — re-dissolved by the same chemistry that built it.

It is preyed upon only at the rim, where the tissue is soft. The mineral mass itself is functionally unattackable; predators take the calendar's newest day and never the years beneath it.

## Plates

**Habitus — lateral section, gravity-true.** The colony is read bottom-up as time: Shelf I is this season's foot, widest and most active; shelves II–IV are older, narrower, the lumen channels closing as old growth seals itself off. The cut face on the left exposes those dead chambers; the intact crust on the right shows only the closed mineral mass. The methane train on the right margin and the barren brine void at top encode the orientation fact: energy comes from below, so the plate gets emptier as it rises — the negative space is the dead direction. ## Easily Confused With

- **Greater hallwright, *Lithophaga aedificatrix*.** The defining confusion of the genus, and the one most worth getting right. Both are sessile *Lithophaga* whose dead works outlast them and shelter tenants. The hallwright builds **outward and enclosing** — its halls are passages, made to be entered, and its living rind wraps a growing tip. *L. gradata* builds **upward and sealing** — its terraces are platforms, made to be climbed and then closed off. A hallwright's interior is corridor; a terrace-builder's interior is dead room. A surveyor who has crawled a hallwright's hall will instinctively look for a way *into* a terrace-builder and find none. The simplest field test: hallwright voids connect; *gradata* voids do not.

- **A fossil hallwright weathered into shelves.** Erosion can step a dead hallwright's crust into terrace-like ledges, and the resulting form mimics *gradata* closely. Distinguish by the laminae: *gradata*'s growth bands run continuous through each shelf and terminate cleanly at the rim, recording deposition. Eroded hallwright ledges show truncated, discordant banding — structure cut across, not laid down. One is a record of building; the other is a record of loss.

- **Mere carbonate terracing (abiotic seep precipitate).** Cold seeps deposit stepped carbonate without any animal at all. The diagnostic difference is the **biofilm gradient and the void lumen**. Abiotic terraces are solid and uniformly filmed or unfilmed; *gradata* shows the sheen thinning upward and hollow chambers within. If the shelves are full and the slope is evenly greasy, suspect chemistry, not life. If the upper shelves are dry and the cut face is hollow, the slope has been alive.

- **The seep foot of a living *gradata*, mistaken for a separate juvenile organism.** The active rim, when the colony is young and low, sits close to the rubble and resembles a small independent film-former. It is not. It is the same animal, simply at the beginning of its climb. Surveyors have catalogued the foot and the tower as two species more than once. They are one organism reading at two ages.
